A transport management system (TMS) is the software that coordinates moving goods: which orders travel on which vehicles, in what sequence, driven by whom, and what happened to each of them. If that sounds broad, it is; the useful way to understand a TMS is by what it replaces.

## What a TMS replaces

Most delivery operations grow into the same stack without choosing it: an inbox where orders arrive, a spreadsheet where someone builds routes, a group chat where execution is coordinated, and a drawer where paper proof accumulates. Each piece works. The system as a whole leaks.

The leak is reconciliation. Someone spends part of every day matching what was planned against what happened: chasing missing proof, rebuilding yesterday's routes from messages, explaining to a customer what the spreadsheet cannot show. That work produces nothing; it only recovers information the operation already had and lost between tools.

A TMS replaces the stack with one record per order that survives the whole cycle: received, planned, dispatched, delivered, proven.

## The signs you need one

Company size is a poor signal; operations with three vans can drown in coordination while larger ones coast on simple, repetitive routes. Better signals:

- **Daily reconciliation work exists.** If matching plan to reality is someone's routine, the tools are leaking.
- **Proof is slow.** If answering "was this delivered, and who signed?" takes more than a minute, disputes and invoicing are paying the cost.
- **Knowledge lives in one person.** If routes depend on what one planner holds in their head, every holiday is an operational risk.
- **Customers ask you for status.** If "where is my order?" arrives by phone, visibility is missing at both ends.

## What changes with a TMS

Planning compresses from hours to minutes because [route optimization](/solutions/route-planning/) computes what was previously assembled by hand. Execution becomes visible because [live tracking](/features/live-tracking/) replaces the call to the driver. And the operation becomes auditable, because every stop ends with [electronic proof of delivery](/features/proof-of-delivery/) on the order record.

Electronic proof of delivery, usually shortened to ePOD, is the digital record that closes a delivery: who received the parcel, when, where, and the evidence that it happened. Instead of a signature on a paper note that rides around in a van, the proof is captured on the driver's phone and lands in the office system the moment the stop completes.

## What an ePOD actually contains

A complete ePOD ties several pieces of evidence to one order:

- **A signature**, drawn on the driver's screen, with the receiver's name typed alongside it.
- **A photo**, for doorstep drops, damage records or "left in a safe place" situations.
- **A barcode or QR scan**, confirming the physical parcel matches the order before anything is signed.
- **A timestamp and location**, recorded automatically rather than written by hand.

The detail that matters is the linkage. Each piece of evidence attaches to the original order record, so an invoice query six weeks later is answered by opening the order, not by searching a filing box.

## Why operations switch from paper

Paper delivery notes fail in two mundane ways. They go missing: the one note that matters is the one that never made it back from the cab. And they arrive late: proof that exists on paper in a van is invisible to the office until the van returns, which means disputes, invoicing and customer questions all wait.

ePOD removes both failure modes. The record cannot be lost because it is never a physical object, and it is available instantly because syncing is automatic. Operations that invoice on delivery feel this immediately: billing can run the same day, backed by evidence.

## What to look for in an ePOD system

Three properties separate systems that work at the doorstep from systems that only work in demos:

1. **Offline capture.** Depots, basements and rural lanes drop signal. The proof must be captured locally and synced later, without the driver noticing.
2. **Speed at the stop.** Every extra tap is multiplied by every stop on every route. Signature, photo and scan should take seconds inside the normal route flow.
3. **One record per order.** Proof that lands in a separate app or inbox recreates the filing-box problem digitally. The POD belongs on the order, next to the route it ran.
